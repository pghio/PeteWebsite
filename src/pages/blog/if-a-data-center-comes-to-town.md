If a data center comes to town, your electric bill should go down.

That is the whole argument.

It is also close enough to happening that our failure to demand it feels less like caution than a lack of imagination.

In March 2026, Amazon, Google, Meta, Microsoft, OpenAI, Oracle, and xAI signed the White House's voluntary [Ratepayer Protection Pledge](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/ratepayer-protection-pledge/). They agreed to build, bring, or buy the new power their data centers require, pay for the delivery infrastructure, and keep paying for it even if they use less than they expected.

Good.

That is the floor.

Now finish the bargain. If a company wants to place an enormous industrial load beside a town, the people who live there should not merely be protected from a higher bill. They should receive a lower one.

One side of the data-center debate says stop. The other says trust.

I want a third verb.

**Bargain.**

Build the data centers. Make them earn it.

This is no longer an abstract argument. In July 2026, New York paused permits for large data centers for up to a year. Governor Kathy Hochul said “progress shouldn't arrive with a higher utility bill.” The Data Center Coalition warned that investment and jobs would “flow elsewhere.” ([Associated Press](https://apnews.com/article/c1e05b74208a6c570eec7c658ac8f187))

There is truth inside both warnings. A bad deal is not progress. A blanket pause eventually becomes policy by surrender. The leverage exists precisely because the companies want to build. Use it.

Here is the compact. The developer brings enough new, dependable, low-carbon power to cover the facility's full consumption and peak demand. It pays every grid cost it creates. Every household in the host municipality—and any adjoining utility service area the regulator finds materially affected—gets a 25 percent credit on ordinary utility charges, through its meter or a mandatory tenant payment. The boundary is fixed before negotiation begins and includes renters, master-metered buildings, and future residents. Power, water, work, and money all go into a public ledger.

## A factory made of servers

A data center arrives twice.

First as a promise: jobs, investment, tax revenue, technological leadership.

Then as a utility bill, a water permit, a substation, and, in the largest cases, millions of square feet of windowless buildings at the edge of town.

The first arrival is narrated by the developer. The second is lived by everyone else. The distance between the two is where trust dies.

That distrust is not irrational. Americans have watched companies privatize the gain and leave communities holding the cleanup bill before. We have watched factories close after receiving subsidies, utilities place private infrastructure into the public rate base, and economic-development projections quietly disappear once the ribbon was cut.

Calling people NIMBYs does not answer that history.

But distrust can harden into its own kind of fantasy.

A data center is not a hole into which electricity and water disappear for no reason. It is a factory. Its raw material is electricity. Its output is computation: the increasingly essential capacity behind medicine, logistics, scientific research, finance, defense, and nearly every service now describing itself as artificial intelligence.

If we intend to use that capacity—and we plainly do—it has to exist somewhere.

The alternative to building it in America is not a world without data centers. It is a world where somebody else builds them, trains the workers, expands the grid, owns the capacity, and acquires the leverage that follows.

Not every data center deserves approval. Not every site can support one. But treating all industrial development as contamination is not an environmental policy. It is an outsourcing policy.

For half a century, Americans became very good at consuming industrial output while treating the places that produced it as somebody else's problem. The factories did not leave because neighbors complained. Automation, trade policy, labor arbitrage, capital allocation, and corporate choices did most of that work; the research on the [China shock](https://www.nber.org/papers/w21906) alone should prevent any one-cause story. But we absorbed a strange moral habit along the way: wanting the product while regarding the act of making it as a failure.

We should not repeat that mistake with computation.

Nor should we repeat the original mistake of industrialization: asking a town to absorb real costs in exchange for benefits that exist mostly in a consultant's spreadsheet.

The answer is not unconditional approval.

It is a contract.

## Start with the water, because everyone else does

The debate usually begins with a large number and no denominator.

A data center can use millions of gallons of water. This is true. A golf course can, too. This is also true. Neither statement tells you whether a proposed facility fits the place where somebody wants to build it.

Here is the closest honest one-facility comparison I could make.

The median American 18-hole golf facility applies **83.9 acre-feet of irrigation water a year**, according to the latest national survey from the Golf Course Superintendents Association of America. That is **27.3 million gallons**, or about **74,900 gallons a day**. The figure describes a median 18-hole facility, not a desert resort or a nine-hole municipal course. ([GCSAA report, Table 39](https://www.gcsaa.org/docs/default-source/what-we-do/gcep-phase-4-water-report.pdf?sfvrsn=c829dd3e_0))

There is no honest national "average data center." A server room and a hyperscale AI campus share a noun and little else. The [International Energy Agency notes](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/understanding-the-energy-ai-nexus) that an AI-focused hyperscale facility can draw 100 megawatts or more, but that does not make 100 megawatts an average. So I am using a defined scenario: a facility with a **100-megawatt maximum draw at the utility meter**, operating at half that level on average, with a power usage effectiveness of 1.40. That leaves an average IT load of about 35.7 megawatts. The 50 percent load is an assumption, not an industry average.

Berkeley Lab's 2024 report models the 2023 hyperscale category at a median **0.32 liters of direct onsite water consumption per IT kilowatt-hour**. In an explicit sensitivity test, that median rises to **0.40**. Apply those cases to the scenario above and you get approximately **26 to 33 million gallons a year** onsite. At the full 100-megawatt utility draw, with the same overhead, it is **53 to 66 million**. These are not measurements from a representative 100-megawatt AI campus. They are a transparent construction from the best national model I could find. ([LBNL report](https://doi.org/10.71468/P1WC7Q))

![National benchmarks show a defined 100 MW data-center scenario using about one golf facility's annual water while supporting about 50 times the economic output and 20 times the jobs](/images/posts/data-center-golf-water.svg)

So the clean headline is not that a golf course uses more water. In this defined scenario, the annual volumes are almost identical: the data center uses **0.97 to 1.21 golf facilities** of onsite water at half load. At full utility draw, it uses **1.9 to 2.4**.

Now ask what the water produces.

The American Golf Industry Coalition's latest national study attributes **$37.1 billion in direct output** and **$53.4 billion in indirect and induced output** to golf-facility operations across **13,946 facilities**. That works out to roughly **$6.5 million in annual economic output** and **64 jobs supported** per facility. These are national averages across every facility class, not the median 18-hole course in the water row. ([AGIC/NGF 2022 Impact Report](https://www.ngf.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/AGIC_Economic-Impact-Report-2023.pdf))

A [2026 Cushman & Wakefield model](https://www.cushmanwakefield.com/en/united-states/insights/from-megawatts-to-multipliers) estimates that each 100 megawatts of new data-center development supports **$334 million in annual gross output**, **$187 million in gross regional product**, and nearly **1,300 jobs** in the local economy. Against the golf-facility operating average, that is about **50 times the economic output** and **20 times the jobs**.

Those are models, not promises. The jobs include supply-chain and downstream work, not 1,300 people walking into the server building every morning. Gross output is not profit, and the two studies did not model the same town in the same year. But the orders of magnitude are real enough to change the argument. This is not a choice between using water and not using water. It is a choice about what a community gets for it.

But there is a wrinkle important enough to put directly under the chart. Golf reports water *applied*. Some becomes plant growth and evaporation; some can return through runoff or groundwater recharge. Berkeley Lab reports data-center water *consumed onsite*, primarily in cooling. Matching withdrawal to withdrawal would generally make the data-center figure larger. Matching consumption to consumption would generally make the golf figure smaller. The data-center number also excludes water consumed wherever its electricity is generated; without a location and power mix, adding a national grid average would create false precision.

That makes the picture useful as a scale comparison, not a claim that the two water accounts are hydrologically identical.

That still does not decide the permit.

Thirty million gallons drawn from a water-rich system with reclaimed supply is a different proposition from the same number pulled from a stressed aquifer. Recycled water should be reported separately, not subtracted as though it ceased to be water. And [Berkeley Lab's national report](https://doi.org/10.71468/P1WC7Q) does not establish the comforting claim that the vast majority of data-center water is recycled. The public source data are not complete enough to support it.

That is the first lesson from the numbers: the honest answer is neither *nothing to worry about* nor *stop everything*.

It is: show me this facility, this watershed, this source, this meter, and this plan for August.

Trust is not a vibe.

It is a meter.

## Bring the power

Water is visible. Electricity is where the larger bargain lives.

Now stress the grid with a tougher operating case. A facility drawing 100 megawatts from the grid at a 90 percent average load consumes **788,400 megawatt-hours a year**. Using national fleet-average capacity factors, matching that annual number would take roughly 388 megawatts of solar nameplate capacity, 262 megawatts of wind, or 99 megawatts of nuclear before delivery losses. Real projects vary by location, vintage, curtailment, and transmission. ([EIA solar, wind, and nuclear capacity factors](https://www.eia.gov/electricity/annual/html/epa_04_08_b.html))

But annual equality is not reliability. A solar farm can make every megawatt-hour the data center uses over a year and still provide nothing when the servers run at two in the morning.

"Bring your own power" has to mean four things at once.

The energy must be **new**: not an operating plant renamed in a press release, and not a resource the utility had already funded for everyone else.

It must be **deliverable**: connected in the relevant part of the grid, shown to reach the facility in the utility's network model, and backed by the required interconnection and transmission rights—not a financial promise three grids away.

It must be **dependable**: enough power the grid operator can count on, plus storage and reserves, to serve the data center at the hours that matter—not merely enough annual certificates to make a sustainability chart reach 100 percent.

It must be **low-carbon**: new clean generation paired with dependable backup. Any onsite fossil generation remains subject to emissions limits, public operating-hour data, and the same air-quality law as everything else.

And it must be **fully paid for**: generation, substations, transmission, distribution, congestion, balancing, backup, and the stranded cost if the project is delayed, downsized, or abandoned.

The Department of Energy's own work on [large-load electricity rates](https://www.energy.gov/policy/articles/electricity-rate-designs-large-loads-evolving-practices-and-opportunities) identifies exactly these risks: cost shifting, grid adequacy, and infrastructure left behind after the customer leaves.

None of this requires inventing a new branch of economics. Depending on the state and utility, large industrial customers already face minimum-demand charges, long contracts, collateral, and exit fees. The White House pledge already says hyperscalers should pay whether they use the power or not.

The only new idea is that paying their own bill should not be confused with doing the town a favor.

## The dividend

The average American household paid **$142.26 a month** for electricity in 2024. A 25 percent discount is **$35.57 a month**, or **$426.78 a year**. Across 10,000 residential meters, the community dividend costs **$4.27 million a year**. Across 100,000, it costs **$42.68 million**. ([EIA residential bill data](https://www.eia.gov/electricity/sales_revenue_price/pdf/table_5A.pdf))

Against the 100-megawatt benchmark above at a 50 percent average draw, the 10,000-household credit equals **$9.74 per megawatt-hour** of facility use—just under **one cent per data-center kilowatt-hour**. That is the clean funding mechanism: a sponsor obligation in the tariff or power contract, not a hopeful raid on future tax revenue.

![My proposed host-community deal: in a 100 MW example, a sponsor charge of about one cent per facility kilowatt-hour funds $4.27 million a year, enough to cut average electricity bills by 25 percent for 10,000 nearby households; the hyperscaler gets a predictable path to build while paying for new power and grid costs, and households receive lower bills while hosting the facility's local impacts](/images/posts/data-center-household-deal.svg)

Those are real numbers. They are also small beside the annual power bill of the projects under discussion. A one-gigawatt campus operating at 90 percent load uses 7.884 million megawatt-hours a year. At the 2024 national average industrial electricity price of 8.13 cents per kilowatt-hour, that is roughly **$641 million a year**. A $4.27 million dividend for 10,000 households would equal about **0.7 percent** of that illustrative energy bill. ([EIA industrial electricity price](https://www.eia.gov/electricity/monthly/epm_table_grapher.php?t=epmt_5_03))

That does not make the obligation trivial. At the same national household average, 100,000 meters would cost $42.68 million a year, or $853.6 million over twenty years before inflation and customer growth. The formula has to be priced before approval: 25 percent times the eligible residential utility revenue inside the fixed boundary, secured for the full term. This is why the geography, term, and parent guarantee matter.

Amazon's newest northern Indiana plan is a **$15 billion**, **2.4-gigawatt** buildout. Amazon says its power agreement will create approximately **$1 billion in system savings for existing Indiana customers over 15 years**. That is not a household credit, and a corporate projection is not a regulatory finding. It does show the claimed customer benefit is already large enough to be counted in tens of millions per year. ([Amazon announcement](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/company-news/amazon-15-billion-indiana-data-centers))

Google's proposed Michigan data center is being paired with **2.7 gigawatts of new grid resources** and a **$10 million Energy Impact Fund** for affordability and household efficiency. That fund is not an ongoing right, either. Both examples simply prove that utilities and developers can already structure a benefit for existing customers. ([Google announcement](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/infrastructure-and-cloud/global-network/clean-energy-reliability-michigan/))

The mechanisms are already appearing. What is missing is a rule that turns scattered corporate programs into a right a resident can see on the same piece of paper as the monthly charge.

At first, I wanted a permanent nominal rate lock: whatever electricity cost before the data center arrived, residents would pay 25 percent less forever.

It makes excellent copy and bad policy.

A hurricane ten years later should not become Google's fault because Google built a data center nearby. A fuel shock, a new environmental rule, or a utility's unrelated incompetence should not be assigned to one industrial customer without limit.

The cleaner bargain has two separate protections.

First, a **25 percent credit against ordinary regulated utility charges**: generation or supply where the regulator has jurisdiction, transmission, distribution, fixed charges, and mandatory riders. Taxes, late fees, and optional products are excluded unless the enabling law says otherwise. Not just the per-kilowatt-hour line, because a utility can move costs into fixed fees faster than a legislator can hold a hearing.

Second, a **hold-harmless guarantee**: every cost attributable to the data center stays in the data center's tariff. Residents do not pay for its new plant, wires, reserves, or forecasting error. An independent regulator audits the with-project and without-project cases each year and reconciles the difference.

If a community wants a ceiling beyond that, make it explicit: twenty years, inflation-indexed, backed by a parent guarantee. A real contract has a number, a term, collateral, and somebody to sue.

Why 25 percent?

Not because an equation descended from the sky and declared it optimal. It is a political number: large enough to be felt, bounded enough to be priced, and simple enough that a resident can tell whether the promise was kept.

The discount is not compensation for a precisely measured injury. It is the host community's share of the value created by infrastructure of national importance.

## A jobs program it is not

The industry will want to sell this as a jobs story. Keep the numbers on the same page.

[Amazon projects](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/company-news/amazon-15-billion-indiana-data-centers) its 2.4-gigawatt Indiana buildout will create 1,100 high-skilled jobs. Meta says its Richland Parish campus—described by the company as five gigawatts of compute capacity—should employ more than 1,000 people once operating. Those are meaningful jobs, especially in rural places. They are not employment miracles proportional to the capital being invested. ([Meta announcement](https://about.fb.com/news/2026/07/teachers-local-businesses-win-as-meta-expands-louisiana-data-center/))

The construction work is larger and more immediate: electricians, lineworkers, pipefitters, equipment operators, concrete crews, and cooling technicians. The power buildout creates another layer. Those are exactly the occupations our culture has spent decades treating as the backup plan while simultaneously complaining that young people cannot find durable work.

But even here, count instead of chanting.

Publish construction-worker hours, permanent headcount, median wages, local-hire percentage, apprenticeship seats, and who actually filled them. A promise of 1,000 jobs should remain visible beside the number 1,000 five years later.

Availability without a credible route is not opportunity. Require paid apprenticeships, community-college partnerships, and contract targets with consequences—not a workforce-development grant small enough to disappear into the press release.

The honest economic case is broader than jobs. Data centers can expand the net tax base after incentives. They create long-lived demand for skilled maintenance and power. More importantly, if the contract reserves public value, they can help finance generation and grid infrastructure without asking residents to pay for it.

That is the real opportunity.

Not a thousand people in branded hard hats on announcement day.

A larger local system, paid for by the new industrial customer, that remains useful after the cameras leave.

## We have made this mistake before

There is a version of the industrial past that exists mostly in lobbying decks. The factory arrives, a town flourishes, everyone owns a house, and nobody gets black lung.

That world did not exist.

Mines poisoned water. Mills mangled bodies. Factory owners used company towns and private police. People who resisted were not irrational enemies of progress. Often they were the only people accurately pricing what progress cost.

There is another version of the story that exists in too many zoning meetings. Industry is inherently degrading, production belongs somewhere over the horizon, and a clean community is one that consumes without making.

That world does not exist either.

When production leaves, the pollution may disappear from view. So do the apprenticeships, supplier networks, tacit knowledge, tax base, and the ladder into a middle-class life that does not begin with four years of debt. The product returns in a shipping container. The leverage does not.

The lesson of industrialization is not that communities should accept whatever arrives.

It is that they should bargain while they still have something the industry needs.

Right now, hyperscalers need land, power, water, permits, and legitimacy. Towns have all five.

Do not trade them for a rendering and a promise.

## Put the bargain where everyone can see it

I have been calling this a social contract. That may be too soft.

Social contracts are invoked when nobody wants to identify the actual contract.

This one needs a stack of enforceable documents. Where applicable, a state-regulator-approved large-load tariff and residential-credit rider hold residents harmless and fund the bill credit. A binding development or community-benefits agreement governs jobs and local money. Water, air, and land-use permits impose resource limits. A parent-company guarantee, audit rights, and successor liability keep the promise alive when the tenant, owner, utility executive, mayor, or governor changes.

The public ledger should contain four things.

**Power.** Hourly facility consumption. New generation delivered. Peak demand. Storage losses. Curtailment. Grid-upgrade costs. Every shortfall and the price paid to replace it.

**Water.** Withdrawn, consumed, discharged, potable, reclaimed, and replenished—reported separately, because those words are not synonyms. Monthly peaks matter more than an annual average when the aquifer is lowest in August.

**Work.** Construction hours, permanent jobs, wages, local hires, apprenticeships promised, apprenticeships started, and apprenticeships completed.

**Money.** Tax benefits granted, taxes paid, residential credits funded, infrastructure assigned to the company, and infrastructure placed into the public rate base.

All four should be independently audited and published in a format a person can download. Not a sustainability score. Not an infographic grading the company on standards the company invented.

The measurements should be boring.

Boring is how trust becomes possible.

## What the dividend does not buy

A lower electric bill is not a pollution license.

Some sites should still be rejected. A water-intensive cooling system may not belong above a stressed aquifer. A gas-turbine complex may fail an air-quality limit no matter how generous the household credit. Noise, habitat, emergency planning, and land use remain real constraints.

The community dividend does not replace environmental law. It sits beside it.

There is also a fair objection that cheaper electricity can encourage people to consume more. It may. Keep ordinary conservation programs and tiered rates underneath the credit, but do not bury the guarantee inside a weather model no resident can audit. I would accept some rebound in exchange for a rule this legible: 25 percent of the eligible bill means 25 percent.

Another objection is that a discount amounts to buying permission from poorer communities.

That risk is real. So preserve non-negotiable health and environmental limits, make the vote public, disclose the alternatives, and prevent developers from selecting the governing boundary after seeing which residents are easiest to exclude. Renters count. Master-metered buildings require audited pass-through to tenants or direct tenant payments; otherwise the landlord pockets the benefit. Future residents count, too. The geography is fixed before negotiation begins.

Money cannot make an unsafe project safe.

But refusing residents a material share of a safe project's value does not protect them. It simply preserves the familiar arrangement in which the company receives the upside, the utility receives a new customer, politicians receive an announcement, and the people beside the facility receive an assurance that somebody ran the numbers.

Run them in public.

Then put the result on the bill.

## Build

The data-center argument has been made artificially small.

One side points to a glass of water. The other points to GDP. Both are trying to win before telling the people who live there what, exactly, they get.

They should get enough new delivered energy, after losses, to match the facility's consumption—plus the dependable capacity, reserves, backup, and transmission required at its coincident peak.

They should get useful shared infrastructure they did not finance.

They should get apprenticeships into the work of building and maintaining it.

They should get enforceable environmental limits and public operating data.

And every month, when the utility bill arrives, they should get proof that the bargain is still being kept.

America should build the productive machinery of the next economy. The companies that profit from it should pay its full cost. The communities that host it should receive a direct dividend.

We do not need to choose between fear and credulity.

We can build.

We can make them earn it.

And we can keep the receipts.

If a data center comes to town, your electric bill should go down.

*—Pete*
