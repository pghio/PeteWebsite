If a data center comes to town, your electric bill should go down.

In March 2026, Amazon, Google, Meta, Microsoft, OpenAI, Oracle, and xAI signed the White House's voluntary [Ratepayer Protection Pledge](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/ratepayer-protection-pledge/). They agreed to build, bring, or buy the new power their data centers require, pay for the delivery infrastructure, and keep paying for it even if they use less than they expected.

Good.

That is the floor.

Now finish the bargain. If a company wants to place an enormous industrial load beside a town, residents should not merely be protected from a higher bill. They should receive a lower one.

My opening bid is a 25 percent credit on ordinary utility charges for every household in the host municipality and any adjoining service area a regulator finds materially affected. The developer also pays every incremental grid cost the regulator attributes to the project, supplies enough new energy and dependable capacity to serve it, accepts enforceable environmental limits, and reports its performance publicly. The eligibility boundary is fixed before negotiations and includes renters, master-metered buildings, and future residents.

New York's July 2026 permitting pause shows the choice taking shape. Governor Kathy Hochul said “progress shouldn't arrive with a higher utility bill.” The Data Center Coalition warned that investment would “flow elsewhere.” ([Associated Press](https://apnews.com/article/c1e05b74208a6c570eec7c658ac8f187)) The debate is too often reduced to stopping projects or accepting developers' terms. Towns can set the terms themselves.

Build the data centers. Make them earn it.

## A factory made of servers

A data center arrives twice.

First as a promise: jobs, investment, tax revenue, technological leadership.

Then as a utility bill, a water permit, a substation, and, in the largest cases, millions of square feet of windowless buildings at the edge of town.

The first arrival is narrated by the developer. The second is lived by everyone else. The distance between the two is where trust dies.

Distrust is earned. Americans have watched companies collect subsidies, close plants, and leave public costs behind. Calling people NIMBYs does not answer that history. Nor does romanticizing the industrial past: mines poisoned water, mills mangled bodies, and many communities had little power to price the damage.

But we have acquired another bad habit: wanting industrial output while treating the act of making it as somebody else's problem. Data centers are factories in the limited but important sense that they turn electricity, equipment, and skilled work into computation. We intend to consume that output. Some of it should be built here.

That does not entitle a project to land, water, power, or a permit. It gives us a reason to bargain instead of defaulting to boosterism or prohibition. Automation, trade policy, labor arbitrage, and corporate choices—not neighborhood opposition alone—did most of the work of hollowing out American industry; the research on the [China shock](https://www.nber.org/papers/w21906) should end any simpler story. The useful lesson is narrower: price the costs before the leverage disappears.

## What the golf comparison can actually tell us

Golf is a familiar-impact baseline, not the proposed substitute. Society already accepts it despite a substantial physical footprint. The comparison asks whether unfamiliarity is distorting our sense of scale: irrigation, substations, warehouses, factories, and other large systems recede into the landscape once we know what they are. A windowless server campus arrives looking uniquely ominous before anyone has priced its actual effects.

The median American 18-hole golf facility applies **83.9 acre-feet of irrigation water a year**, according to the latest national survey from the Golf Course Superintendents Association of America. That is **27.3 million gallons**, or about **74,900 gallons a day**. The figure describes a median 18-hole facility, not a desert resort or a nine-hole municipal course. ([GCSAA report, Table 39](https://www.gcsaa.org/docs/default-source/what-we-do/gcep-phase-4-water-report.pdf?sfvrsn=c829dd3e_0))

There is no useful national “average data center.” I use a defined scenario instead: **100 megawatts maximum at the utility meter**, operating at half that level on average, with a power usage effectiveness of 1.40. The 50 percent load is an assumption, not an industry average.

Berkeley Lab's 2024 report models the 2023 hyperscale category at **0.32 liters of direct onsite water consumption per IT kilowatt-hour** and tests **0.40** as a higher case. Applied to this scenario, that is approximately **26 to 33 million gallons a year** onsite; at full meter draw it becomes **53 to 66 million**. These are modeled values, not measurements from a representative 100-megawatt AI campus. The report itself warns that actual cooling performance may miss modeled design performance. ([LBNL report](https://doi.org/10.71468/P1WC7Q))

The apparent match needs to be disclosed before the punchline: golf reports water *applied*; Berkeley Lab reports data-center water *consumed onsite*. Some irrigation returns through runoff or groundwater recharge. The data-center figure excludes water consumed in generating its electricity. Matching withdrawal to withdrawal would generally raise the data-center number; matching consumption to consumption would generally lower the golf number.

![National benchmarks show a defined 100 MW data-center scenario using about one golf facility's annual water while supporting about 50 times the economic output and 20 times the jobs](/images/posts/data-center-golf-water.svg)

The comparison establishes an order of magnitude, not hydrological equivalence and certainly not permission to build. The economic rows are also scale indicators, not forecasts. A national golf-industry model averages roughly **$6.5 million in annual output** and **64 supported jobs** across 13,946 facilities. A 2026 Cushman & Wakefield model estimates **$334 million in gross output**, **$187 million in gross regional product**, and nearly **1,300 direct, supply-chain, and downstream jobs** for 100 megawatts of data-center development. The studies use different years, geographies, and definitions. The chart does not establish a trustworthy national multiplier, and those 1,300 people are not all working inside the building. ([Golf impact report](https://www.ngf.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/AGIC_Economic-Impact-Report-2023.pdf); [data-center model](https://www.cushmanwakefield.com/en/united-states/insights/from-megawatts-to-multipliers))

The harder comparison is local. What other project might use the same transmission capacity, land, or water? How much tax revenue remains after abatements? How many permanent jobs and how much payroll stay nearby? Regulators should publish that opportunity cost and a net fiscal analysis before approval. A project does not deserve priority merely because its gross output is large.

Thirty million gallons from reclaimed supply in a water-rich system is not thirty million from a stressed aquifer in August. Recycled water must be reported separately, not subtracted as though it ceased to be water. Berkeley Lab's national data do not support the claim that most data-center water is recycled.

Show me this facility, this watershed, this source, this meter, and its drought plan.

Trust is not a vibe.

It is a meter.

## Bring the power

Annual energy matching is not reliability. A solar farm can generate as many megawatt-hours as a data center uses over a year and still provide nothing when the servers run at two in the morning.

“Bring your own power” therefore needs a regulator's definition. The supply must be **incremental**, rather than an existing plant renamed in a press release; **deliverable** to the relevant grid; and backed by enough accredited capacity, reserves, storage, and transmission to cover the project's modeled coincident peak. It should be low-carbon, with any fossil backup subject to ordinary air rules and public operating-hour data.

The developer pays every incremental generation, network, balancing, and stranded-asset cost identified by the regulator, with annual true-ups as forecasts meet reality. It also accepts emergency curtailment terms rather than making households the shock absorber. The Department of Energy already identifies cost shifting, grid adequacy, and stranded infrastructure as the central [large-load rate-design risks](https://www.energy.gov/policy/articles/electricity-rate-designs-large-loads-evolving-practices-and-opportunities). The White House pledge accepts the principle that hyperscalers pay even when their demand falls. A permit should make it enforceable; paying their own bill is not doing the town a favor.

## The dividend

The average American household paid **$142.26 a month** for electricity in 2024. A 25 percent discount is **$35.57 a month**, or **$426.78 a year**. Across 10,000 residential meters, the community dividend costs **$4.27 million a year**. Across 100,000, it costs **$42.68 million**. ([EIA residential bill data](https://www.eia.gov/electricity/sales_revenue_price/pdf/table_5A.pdf))

Against the 100-megawatt benchmark at a 50 percent average draw, the 10,000-household credit equals **$9.74 per megawatt-hour** of facility use—just under **one cent per data-center kilowatt-hour**. Compared with the 2024 national average industrial electricity price of 8.13 cents, the credit alone would add about 12 percent before the separate cost of new power and grid work. The company would notice, which is the point. ([EIA industrial electricity price](https://www.eia.gov/electricity/monthly/epm_table_grapher.php?t=epmt_5_03))

![My proposed host-community deal: in a 100 MW example, a sponsor charge of about one cent per facility kilowatt-hour funds $4.27 million a year, enough to cut average electricity bills by 25 percent for 10,000 nearby households; the hyperscaler gets a predictable path to build while paying for new power and grid costs, and households receive lower bills while hosting the facility's local impacts](/images/posts/data-center-household-deal.svg)

The one-cent figure is not the funding formula; otherwise a half-empty facility could starve a fixed household promise. The obligation is 25 percent of eligible residential charges, funded through a sponsor payment with a guaranteed annual floor, inflation adjustment, regulatory true-up, and parent-company backing. If the eligible population or local bill changes, the obligation changes. Price it before approval.

At first I wanted a permanent nominal rate lock. It makes excellent copy and bad policy. A hurricane or fuel shock ten years later should not become one data center's fault.

The workable version has two protections. The **dividend** is a 25 percent credit against ordinary regulated charges, including fixed charges and mandatory riders, not taxes, late fees, or optional products. The **hold-harmless rule** keeps every project-attributable cost in the data center's tariff. An independent regulator compares the grid with and without the project and reconciles the difference each year.

Why 25 percent?

It is an opening bid, not an optimized national answer: large enough to notice, simple enough to audit, and bounded enough to price. A small facility beside a large city may not support it; a giant campus in a tiny jurisdiction might owe more. If the compact makes a project uneconomic after its full local cost is counted, that may be the wrong project or the wrong site.

## Count the jobs honestly

[Amazon says](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/company-news/amazon-15-billion-indiana-data-centers) its planned $15 billion, 2.4-gigawatt Indiana buildout will create 1,100 high-skilled jobs. Meaningful, especially in a rural area; still a company projection, and not an employment miracle proportional to the capital involved. Construction creates more immediate demand for electricians, lineworkers, pipefitters, equipment operators, concrete crews, and cooling technicians.

The contract should separate temporary construction jobs, permanent onsite jobs, and modeled spillovers. Publish hours, wages, local hires, and filled positions. Require paid apprenticeships and community-college routes with targets and penalties. A promise of 1,000 jobs should remain visible beside the actual number five years later.

Hyperscalers need land, power, water, permits, and legitimacy. Towns have all five. Do not trade them for a rendering and a promise.

## Put the bargain where everyone can see it

“Social contract” is too soft. This requires a regulator-approved large-load tariff and residential-credit rider, a binding development agreement, ordinary environmental permits, a parent guarantee, audit rights, automatic damages for missed payments, and successor liability when the owner or tenant changes.

The public ledger has four columns: **power** consumed, delivered, and paid for; **water** withdrawn, consumed, discharged, potable, and reclaimed; **work** promised and actually performed; and **money** granted, paid, credited, or shifted into the public rate base. Report monthly peaks, not just annual averages. Publish the underlying data, not a sustainability score.

The measurements should be boring. That is how residents verify the deal.

## What the dividend does not buy

A lower electric bill is not a pollution license.

Some sites should still be rejected. A water-intensive cooling system may not belong above a stressed aquifer. A gas-turbine complex may fail an air-quality limit no matter how generous the household credit. Noise, habitat, emergency planning, and land use remain real constraints.

The community dividend does not replace environmental law. It sits beside it.

Another objection is that a discount amounts to buying permission from poorer communities.

That risk is real. The project must clear non-negotiable health and environmental limits before money enters the decision. Make the vote public, disclose the alternatives, and prevent developers from drawing the boundary around residents easiest to exclude. Renters count. Master-metered buildings require audited pass-through or direct tenant payments. Future residents count.

Money cannot make an unsafe project safe.

For a project that is independently found safe, refusing residents a material share does not protect them. It preserves the familiar arrangement in which the company gets the upside, the utility gets a customer, politicians get an announcement, and neighbors get an assurance that somebody ran the numbers.

Run the numbers in public, then put the result on the bill.

America should build more of the computing infrastructure it increasingly relies on. Its owners should pay every incremental local cost, and host communities should receive a dividend they can see each month.

Build the data centers. Make them earn it.

If one comes to town, your electric bill should go down.
