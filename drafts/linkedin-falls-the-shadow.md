=== POST ===

In 2016, the godfather of AI said we should stop training radiologists. "It's just completely obvious" that deep learning would beat them within five years.

A decade later: radiologist pay hit $571K, up 9% in a year. Residency positions hit an all-time record.

And the Mayo Clinic, which runs 250+ AI models inside its radiology department, employs 55% MORE radiologists than it did in 2016. The hospital that adopted AI hardest hired the most humans.

Here's what the forecast missed. Reading a scan is two jobs wearing one coat: spotting the pattern, and judging what it means. The machine took the pattern half. Scans got cheaper, so medicine ordered more, and demand for judgment went up, not down.

Writing is the same two jobs: producing the prose, and meaning something by it. The prose half just became free. Everything now rides on the half that's left.

I benchmarked 8 LLMs on my product's real workload this year, from $0.15/M tokens to 100x that:

→ On clear requests, price bought almost nothing. Every model did fine.
→ On ambiguous requests, every model hit the same wall. Cheap and expensive alike.
→ The smartest behavior I observed wasn't an answer. It was a model declining to guess and asking a better question.

The frontier of machine intelligence is a question pointed back at you: what do you mean?

Intelligence became abundant. Intention did not.

One more thing worries me, though. Writing was never just the output of thinking. It was the apparatus for it. Hand every draft to the machine and you skip the collisions that turn a notion into an intention. Your asks get vaguer, the output gets more generic, you hand over more. Each lap it tightens.

So here's a bet, on the record while the data still runs against me: the English major is coming back. Close reading and revision are the oldest training we have for the two skills machines just made precious — checking what a sentence actually commits to, and figuring out what you actually mean.

Hold me to it: within a decade, the decline in English majors reverses.
