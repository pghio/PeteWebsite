# What the Fire Was For

Inside your skull are roughly a hundred billion neurons, firing without pause, running a model of the world so fast and so seamless that you mistake it for the world itself. You did not build it. You cannot switch it off. It is, by a wide margin, the most sophisticated object anyone has ever found.

We have decided to open the conversation about artificial intelligence from the premise that this machine is about to be embarrassed.

I want to open somewhere else.

---

The case against AI gets filed under fear of the unknown, which is the lazy reading. The fear is not vague. It is exact. For three years we have told people, in demo after launch, that the model passed the exam, wrote the essay, shipped the app, read the scan. Those sentences share a grammatical subject, and it is never you.

People are not afraid of the tool. They are afraid of the sentence we keep wrapping around it.

It would be easy to call that hype, or arrogance. I think it is something more forgivable and more structural. The industry communicates *replacement* because replacement is the only thing it can measure. "Scored in the 90th percentile on the bar exam" is a number. "Made a good lawyer better at the one part of the work that is irreducibly hers" is not a number — and a field that lives and dies by benchmarks will say the thing its benchmarks can express. The marketing failure is downstream of a measurement failure. We are not lying to people. We are telling them the only thing our instruments can see.

---

Underneath all of it is a confusion about friction.

We discuss technology as though its purpose were to remove friction, and progress as though it were friction trending toward zero. But no tool in history has removed effort. It relocates it. The plow did not abolish the labor of eating; it moved that labor from the hunt to the field, and later to the office, and lately to a screen. Each tool picks friction up and sets it down somewhere else, and asks whether we prefer it there.

So the honest question about AI was never whether it removes friction. It is *where it moves the friction* — and whether that was a place the friction was quietly doing something we needed.

Because friction isn't one thing.

![Two kinds of friction — the toil worth removing and the forge worth keeping](/images/posts/friction-two-kinds.svg)

There is the friction of **toil**: drudgery, boilerplate, the second draft of the same form, the invisible work of remembering the milk and the permission slip and the dentist. Remove it and you have done someone a kindness. That is the real promise, and it is large.

And there is the friction of the **forge**: the difficulty that has nowhere to discharge except into you. Cognitive scientists have a sober name for it — *desirable difficulty*, the conditions that make performance slower now and learning deeper later. It is the resistance that turns into judgment, the repetition that turns into taste, the struggle that hardens into a self. Remove it and you have not done a kindness. You have performed a theft so quiet the victim applauds.

Here is the quiet center of the whole essay: **on a dashboard, the two look identical.** Both read as time spent, cost incurred, steps not yet automated. I build measurement systems for a living, and I trust them precisely because I know what they are blind to. Optimization can see toil and cannot see the forge, so left to its own logic it removes both — and reports the second removal as an improvement. The forge never shows up as the forge. It shows up as inefficiency.

---

I have one rule, and I held it long before any of this. **Be impatient with your inputs. Be patient with your results.**

Inputs are the work you actually control: the draft, the rep, the question asked precisely instead of vaguely. Results are a lagging, noisy function of those inputs and a world that does not consult you. Impatience is a fine instrument when you point it at something you can change, and a corrosive one when you point it at something you can't. Aimed at inputs, impatience is called focus. Aimed at results, it is called anxiety.

The frictionless pitch trains the exact opposite reflex. It offers results at the speed of a keystroke and invites you to stop minding the inputs at all. Be impatient for everything; let the machine mind the work. It is my rule, run precisely backward.

The strange part is that my rule is also how you use these tools *well*. Everything I have learned building with them is input discipline wearing work clothes: ambiguity is expensive, so be specific; a fresh context window is like handing the model eight hours of sleep; build the good tools first and suffer less forever. Used well, AI does not lower the standard on your inputs. It clears the toil off the desk so you can put all of yourself into the inputs that were the point.

And the inputs are the point in a way that is not a metaphor. Your brain consolidates what it worked for; the difficulty was doing the encoding all along. An answer you did not struggle for is a fact you are renting. A library of outputs you cannot reproduce is not mastery — it is dependency wearing mastery's clothes.

![Where growth lives — the inverted-U of friction against who you become](/images/posts/friction-growth-curve.svg)

Put effort on one axis and who you become on the other, and you do not get a line that climbs as effort falls. You get an arch. Too little friction and we soften; too much and we break; we become ourselves in the band between. "Frictionless" sells you a seat at the far left of that curve and calls it freedom.

---

This is the part the metrics will always get wrong, because the part that matters never survives as a metric.

The result is what gets recorded: the company that sold or didn't, the exam passed, the project shipped. But the result is rarely what changed you. The struggle was. Ask people about the work that made them who they are, and they won't describe the outcome — they'll describe the stretch they weren't sure they'd come through. The outcome is what the world keeps. The friction is what you keep.

Which is why *let the machine handle the hard part* is a stranger offer than it sounds. The hard part was the part that was yours.

---

So I am not anti-AI. I am the opposite, loudly, and I build with it daily. I only want us to stop selling it with the one sentence guaranteed to make people hate it and themselves.

AI's real gift was never a life without struggle. It is the chance to spend your struggle on what is actually yours — and to hand the rest, at last, to something that doesn't mind carrying it.

Be impatient with your inputs. Be patient with your results. Let the machine take the toil.

And leave you the fire.

*—Pete*
