# In What Furnace

Most of the worry about AI gets filed under fear of the unknown. I don't think that's what it is. The fear is specific, and it's mostly a reaction to how the thing is being sold.

For a few years the pitch has been a list of jobs the machine can do instead of you. It passed the bar. It wrote the essay. It shipped the app, read the scan, made the call. Every headline has the same subject, and the subject is never the person reading it. Tell people long enough that a tool exists to replace them and you shouldn't be surprised when they hate the tool.

I don't think that's arrogance, or not only. "It replaced a lawyer" is a claim you can measure. "It made a lawyer better at the part of the work that's actually hers" is not. So we say the first one, because there's a number attached. The whole pitch ends up shaped by what's easy to count, and what's easy to count is replacement.

The deeper mistake underneath it is treating friction as a single thing.

We describe technology as if its purpose is to remove effort, and progress as if it's effort heading toward zero. But effort doesn't vanish when a tool shows up. It moves. The plow didn't end the labor of eating; it pushed that labor out into the field, and later into an office, and lately onto a screen. Every tool picks the difficulty up and puts it down somewhere else. The only real question is where it put it, and whether that was a place you wanted it gone from.

Some friction is just toil. The second pass on a form. Remembering the milk and the permission slip. The forty minutes of setup before ten minutes of actual work. Take that off someone's plate and you've done them a genuine favor. That's most of what people mean when they say AI is going to help, and on that they're right.

But some friction is what builds the person. You don't get judgment until you've been wrong a few hundred times. You don't get taste without the dull repetition that lays it down. The difficulty is doing something to you while you lean on it, and that is the whole point of it. Strip it out and you haven't helped anyone. You've removed the thing that was working.

![Two kinds of friction — the toil worth removing and the forge worth keeping](/images/posts/friction-two-kinds.svg)

The problem is that the two look identical from the outside. Both register as time, cost, a step nobody has automated yet. I spend my days measuring systems like this, and the honest thing to say is that the measurement can't tell them apart. It finds toil easily. It has no way to see the friction that was slowly making someone better, so it reads that friction as waste and removes it, and calls the removal progress.

I've held one rule for a long time, since well before any of this. Be impatient with your inputs and patient with your results. The inputs are the part you control: the work you actually put in, the question you bothered to get right. The results are whatever the world decides to do with that, on a schedule you don't set. Pointed at the inputs, impatience is just focus. Pointed at the results, it curdles into anxiety, and the results don't arrive any sooner for it. Most of the unhappiness I've watched people manufacture is impatience aimed at the wrong half.

![Where to aim your impatience — focus on the inputs you control, patience for the results you don't](/images/posts/impatience-inputs-results.svg)

Frictionless AI sells the opposite habit. It offers the result now and tells you the inputs are the machine's problem, not yours. That's the rule running backward. What's strange is that when I actually use these tools well, I end up doing the reverse of what the pitch promises. I'm more careful with the inputs, not less, because the tool cleared away the busywork and handed me back the part that needs judgment.

Draw the relationship and you don't get a line that climbs as effort drops. You get a hump. Too little friction and you go slack. Too much and you break. The person you're trying to become shows up somewhere in the middle. "Frictionless" is a marketing word for the far left of that curve, sold to you as freedom.

![Where growth lives — the hump of friction against who you become](/images/posts/friction-growth-curve.svg)

Almost none of the part that matters survives as a number. The result is what gets written down: the company that sold, the grade, the thing that launched. But the result is rarely what changed you. Ask anyone about the work that made them who they are and they won't describe how it turned out. They'll describe the stretch where they weren't sure it would. That stretch is exactly what the frictionless pitch is offering to take off your hands, and it was the only part that was ever yours.

So I'm not against AI. I use it every day. I only want us to stop selling it on the one promise that turns people against it, which is the promise to take over the work that mattered. Give the machine the toil. Keep the rest. The rest was never slowing you down by accident.

—Pete
